Z-Scores for Stylometry

A step-by-step walkthrough: raw counts → relative frequencies → z-scores → document similarity

Step 1 The Raw Data: Word Counts

Counting Function Words

Stylometrists typically analyse the most frequent function words — grammatical words like 的, 了, 是, 他, 有. Unlike content words, function words are used unconsciously and are hard for an author to deliberately control, making them strong markers of individual style. The Problem with Raw Counts

Documents have different lengths. A 10,000-word novel will naturally have more occurrences of 的 than a 2,000-word essay, even if both authors use the word at the same rate. We cannot compare raw counts directly — we first need to normalize them.

Step 2 Normalize to Relative Frequencies

Removing the Effect of Document Length

We divide each raw count by the document’s total word count. The result is a relative frequency (a proportion between 0 and 1), which makes documents of different lengths comparable. Below the data rows, the mean and standard deviation of each column are shown — these are the key ingredients for z-score standardization.

Step 3 Z-Score Standardization

Putting Everything on the Same Scale

The z-score tells us how many standard deviations a value is above or below the mean. After standardization, every column has mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1, regardless of the original scale.

Reading Z-Scores

Positive z-score: the value is above the mean for that word. The document uses this word more than average.

Negative z-score: the value is below the mean. The document uses this word less than average.

z = 0: exactly at the mean.

Now all features are comparable: a z-score of +2 for 的 and +2 for 有 both mean “2 standard deviations above average” — even though the underlying frequencies were very different.

An author’s profile is the pattern of green and red cells across the row. Documents that share this pattern are likely written in the same style, or by the same author.

Why This Matters for Stylometry

  • After z-scoring, each word contributes equally to distance measures and downstream analysis (like PCA or clustering).
  • A word that an author uses at an unusual rate can now “stand out” just as much as any other word’s pattern.
