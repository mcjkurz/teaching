Z-Score Standardization

A step-by-step walkthrough of how z-scores put different features on the same scale

Step 1 The Raw Data: Word Frequencies

Stylometry: Identifying Authors by Word Usage

In stylometry, we count how often each word appears in a document. Different words have very different frequencies — common words like 的 appear hundreds of times, while rarer words like 面包 might appear just a handful of times. This difference in scale makes it hard to compare features directly.

Notice the Scale Problem

Look at the values: 的 ranges from roughly 40–90, while 面包 ranges from 0–5. If we tried to compare documents directly, the high-frequency word 的 would dominate and drown out the signal from rarer words. We need a way to put all features on the same scale.

Step 2 Column Mean & Standard Deviation

Summarizing Each Feature

For each word (column), we computed two statistics — now visible as the two colored rows at the bottom of the table: the mean (average count across all documents) and the standard deviation (how spread out the counts are). These two numbers are the key ingredients for z-score standardization.

The Formulas

Mean (average value in a column):

$$\mu_j = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{ij}$$

Standard Deviation (spread around the mean):

$$\sigma_j = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{ij} - \mu_j)^2}$$

Here $j$ is the column (word) and $i$ is the row (document). We use the population standard deviation (dividing by $n$) for simplicity.

Step 3 Z-Score Standardization

Putting Everything on the Same Scale

The z-score tells us how many standard deviations a value is above or below the mean. After standardization, every column has mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1, regardless of the original scale.

The Z-Score Formula

$$z_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij} - \mu_j}{\sigma_j}$$

Subtract the column mean, then divide by the column standard deviation. This centers the data (mean → 0) and rescales it (std dev → 1).

Reading Z-Scores

Positive z-score: the value is above the mean for that word. The document uses this word more than average.

Negative z-score: the value is below the mean. The document uses this word less than average.

z = 0: exactly at the mean.

Now all features are comparable: a z-score of +2 for 的 and a z-score of +2 for 面包 both mean "2 standard deviations above average" — even though the raw counts were vastly different.

Why This Matters for Stylometry

  • After z-scoring, each word contributes equally to distance measures and downstream analysis (like PCA or clustering).
  • A rare word that an author uses unusually often can now "stand out" just as much as a common word pattern.
  • Try editing the raw data in Step 1 and watch how the z-scores change!

Step 4 Document Similarity

Comparing Documents with Cosine Similarity

Now that every feature is on the same scale, we can meaningfully compare documents. Cosine similarity measures how similar the direction of two vectors is, ignoring their magnitude. A value of 1 means identical direction (very similar), 0 means unrelated, and −1 means opposite.

Cosine Similarity Formula

$$\text{sim}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\| \, \|\mathbf{b}\|} = \frac{\sum_j a_j b_j}{\sqrt{\sum_j a_j^2} \;\sqrt{\sum_j b_j^2}}$$

We compute this for every pair of documents using their z-score vectors. The heatmap below shows the result: brighter/warmer colors mean the documents are more similar.

Reading the Heatmap

  • The diagonal is always 1.00 (each document is perfectly similar to itself).
  • Look for the brightest off-diagonal cells — those are the most similar document pairs.
  • Low or negative similarity means the documents have very different word-usage patterns.
